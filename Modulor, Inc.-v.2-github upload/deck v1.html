§02 / Problem03 / 12
What's missing

Force plates measure power. GPS measures output. HRV measures recovery.
Nothing measures muscle.

Strength & conditioning has spent fifteen years instrumenting outputs — watts, velocities, heart-rate variability, sprint splits.

The tissue that actually tears — hamstrings, adductors, quads, calves — is still measured by hand, by feel, by clinician estimate.

Two athletes can present with the same goniometer reading and have radically different injury risk. There is no instrument, no unit, no record.

§03 / Product04 / 12
The Machine — VITR

An autonomous stretching platform that measures what it does.

Step 01

Strap In

Athlete steps onto the platform. Quick-release cuffs. Clinician selects the protocol. Under a minute.

Step 02

Stretch

Motor-controlled cables ramp force at 0.5 lb/sec, hard-capped at 30 lb. PID compliance backs off the moment the athlete resists.

Step 03

Measure

Inline load cells at 100 Hz. Rotary encoders track exact extension. Joint angle to ±2°, asymmetry to ±1.4%.

Step 04

Detect

Overhead depth camera reads skeleton pose. Pelvis rotation, trunk shift, knee bend — flagged in real time, logged.

§05 / The Name06 / 12
Why "Modulor"

In 1945, Le Corbusier published Le Modulor — a system of proportions derived from the human body and tuned to the golden section.

He used it to design buildings that fit people: at the scale of a hand on a banister and an arm raised overhead.

A measure, taken from the body, given back to the body.

Modulor is the measurement system for the tissue performance medicine still estimates by eye.
